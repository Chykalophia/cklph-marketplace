# Voice, Headlines & Microcopy

## The 4 U's headline formula (default test)
Make every headline Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific. Hit at least three of four; "useful + ultra-specific" is the non-negotiable pair.
- Useful: does it promise a clear benefit?
- Urgent: a reason to read or act now?
- Unique: does it say something category norms do not?
- Ultra-specific: numbers, names, concrete detail for credibility.

Weak vs strong:
- Weak: "Strategic consulting services for SMBs."
- Strong: "How Wilburn Medical recovered $340K in stalled revenue in 90 days."

## Headline families (templates)
- Outcome: "{Achieve outcome} without {pain}." / "{Outcome} in {timeframe}."
- Problem: "Never {unpleasant event} again." / "Stop {pain}. Start {pleasure}."
- Audience: "{Product} for {audience} who want {outcome}."
- Differentiation: "The {category} that {key differentiator}."
- Proof: "{Number} {people} use {product} to {outcome}."

## Above-the-fold formula
Headline + subheadline (the how) + primary CTA + supporting visual + optional social-proof bar. The headline carries the promise; the subhead carries the mechanism.

## CTA formula
[Action verb] + [what they get] + [optional qualifier]. "Start my free audit," not "Submit." Match button copy to the value, not the system action.

## Microcopy that reduces purchase anxiety
Place near the CTA: free shipping/returns, a money-back or product guarantee, secure-checkout, and a plain-language expectation ("made to order, ships in 3 to 6 weeks"). Specific reassurance beats vague policy.

## Chykalophia voice rules
- Confident, warm, clear. Treat the reader as smart and busy.
- Simple over complex; active over passive; specific over vague.
- No em dashes. Use commas, colons, periods, or parentheses instead.
- No exclamation-point inflation; let the claim carry the energy.
- No corporate-speak. Banned phrases: leverage, synergy, streamline, best-in-class, world-class, cutting-edge, game-changer, seamless, robust, "in today's world," "it's important to note," "I hope this finds you well."
- Cut hedging: might, could potentially, in some cases, very, really, just.
- One idea per sentence. If a sentence has two ideas, split it.
- Read it aloud. If you stumble, rewrite it.

## Quick self-edit pass
1. Does the first line earn the second? 2. Is there one clear action? 3. Every claim specific and true? 4. Any buzzword, hedge, or em dash to cut? 5. Would the reader know what to do and why in one scan?
