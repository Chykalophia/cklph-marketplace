# Research Playbook

## The loop: recon → plan → fan-out → verify → synthesize

### Recon (sharpen before you spend)
- Look at the actual subject first (the page, the product, the repo, the dataset). Five minutes of recon makes every downstream question more concrete.
- Pull any first-party/primary data the subject owns. This both grounds the work and reveals what's worth researching.
- Use recon findings to write better stream briefs (you'll know the real product, price, audience, edge cases).

### Plan streams (decompose for parallelism)
- Break the topic into **3–5 independent streams** that minimally overlap. Independence is what makes parallel execution efficient and non-redundant.
- Each stream should be answerable on its own and map to a section of the eventual output.
- Give each stream a one-line mandate and the specific outputs it must return (stats with sources, example URLs, a "so-what" per finding).

### Fan out (parallel by default)
- Launch streams concurrently when parallel subagents/workers are available; otherwise run them sequentially with the same briefs.
- Meanwhile, do the **first-party/interactive data pulls yourself** — you want to see those numbers directly because they shape the narrative and the questions.
- Keep streams to a depth budget appropriate to the ask ("spend real effort" vs "quick scan"). For a comprehensive report, deep streams + your own data + verification is the right floor.

### Collect with discipline
- Every statistic: source name + URL + year, captured at collection time (not reconstructed later).
- Capture concrete examples with URLs (so the reader can go look), not just assertions.
- Note the *type* of source as you go (primary study, vendor blog, platform benchmark) — you'll tier them next.

### Verify (adversarial pass)
- Identify the 2–3 claims the conclusion actually rests on. Re-fetch their **primary** sources and confirm the figure verbatim.
- Watch for: figures attributed to an authority by secondary blogs but absent from the primary; self-interested vendor numbers; stale data presented as current; round numbers that smell like estimates.
- Downgrade or drop anything that doesn't survive. It's better to say "directional" than to be wrong.

### Synthesize
- Lead with the conclusion / single most important finding.
- Weave evidence with inline citations; group by theme.
- Where sources conflict, present both and say which you'd act on and why.
- End with a sourced reference list and a one-paragraph reliability note.

## Quality bar
- Prioritize authoritative, recent sources (e.g., for CRO: Baymard, Nielsen Norman Group, Spiegel/Northwestern, Portent, Google, McKinsey, Bain, Statista; plus reputable case studies clearly labeled).
- Aim for genuine depth proportional to the ask. If the user says "spend an hour" or "be comprehensive," that's a mandate for many searches, primary-source fetches, and breadth across every angle.
- The output should let a skeptic check your work — that's the test.
