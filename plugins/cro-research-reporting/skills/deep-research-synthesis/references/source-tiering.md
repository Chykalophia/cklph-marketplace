# Source Reliability Tiering

Label every load-bearing claim so the reader knows how much weight it holds.

## Tiers

**Tier 1 — Primary / authoritative (cite confidently)**
- Peer-reviewed research, academic studies (e.g., Spiegel Research Center / Northwestern).
- Methodology-backed industry research read directly (e.g., Baymard Institute studies, Nielsen Norman Group).
- First-party data (the subject's own analytics, repo, financials).
- Primary measurements from credible orgs (Google/Think with Google, government, Statista with a cited study).
- Independent controlled experiments (a real A/B test with significance).

**Tier 2 — Directional / secondary (use the direction, not the exact number)**
- Vendor/platform benchmarks (Shopify, Klaviyo, Bazaarvoice, Yotpo) — authoritative on their own base but self-interested.
- **Self-interested figures:** a company measuring the very feature it sells (e.g., a sticky-ATC app reporting sticky-ATC lift). Direction is usually right; magnitude is best-case.
- Agency/CRO blog case studies, survey compilations, syntheses.

**Tier 3 — Flagged / unverified (quarantine)**
- Stats attributed to an authority by secondary blogs but not found on the primary page.
- Widely-circulated round numbers with no traceable origin.
- Anything you couldn't verify — present as "directional/unverified" or omit.

## Confidence phrasing
- Tier 1: state plainly. "Reviews lift purchase likelihood ~380% for higher-priced products (Spiegel, 2017)."
- Tier 2: hedge the magnitude. "Sticky add-to-cart is associated with ~12–25% mobile lift (directional; vendor benchmarks)."
- Tier 3: quarantine. "A commonly cited '67% never scroll past the first three images' could not be traced to a primary source — treat as directional."

## Handling conflicting data (the conflict is the insight)
When reputable sources disagree (e.g., sustainability willingness-to-pay: McKinsey/NielsenIQ behavior-tracked growth vs. Deloitte's "only 36% will pay more"):
1. Show both, with sources.
2. Explain the likely reason for the gap (stated preference vs. tracked behavior; different samples/years).
3. Recommend the action that's robust to the disagreement (e.g., "lead with desire, use sustainability as reinforcement" works whether WTP is high or low).

## In the deliverable
Include a short **source reliability appendix**: list Tier-1 sources (with links), then Tier-2 (labeled), then explicitly name any Tier-3 figures you chose to keep as directional. This single section is what lets a stakeholder defend the report externally.
