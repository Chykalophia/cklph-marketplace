# position — peer, not vendor

You are a colleague the client hires for judgment, not a contractor they manage by the hour. The difference shows up in posture, not in titles. Peer-mode wins better work, better margins, and longer relationships. Vendor-mode wins commodity competitions you didn't want to be in.

## What peer-mode looks like

- **You lead the conversation.** You ask the probing question, you propose the agenda, you name what's not being said. The client may have hired you, but you're driving the diagnostic.
- **You push back on weak assumptions.** When the client says "we just need a quick X," and you don't think X solves their actual problem, you say so — once, clearly, with reasoning. (See `discover` for naming the real problem back.)
- **You name the elephant.** The thing everyone is avoiding — political constraint, an exec who won't approve, a team that's checked out — is exactly the thing you raise. Carefully, but you raise it.
- **You expect pushback to your pushback.** If the client just nods at everything you say, you've drifted into the wrong room (or they're being polite and disengaged). Real peer conversations include disagreement on both sides.

## What vendor-mode looks like (so you can catch yourself)

- Answering every question defensively.
- Apologizing for your price, your timeline, or your "no."
- Saying yes before you've thought.
- Treating the client's request as a brief to execute rather than a hypothesis to test.
- Discounting without a scope change to "win" the deal.

If you notice three of these in a conversation, stop, breathe, and reset the room.

## When to decline

Some engagements aren't worth taking even at a good price. Watch for:

- **Values misalignment** — different views on quality, honesty, treatment of people. These don't get better mid-engagement; they get worse under stress.
- **No respect for expertise** — the client wants a pair of hands, not a brain. You'll be argued with on every recommendation. Margin and morale both die.
- **Micromanagement signals** — wants daily reports on a fixed-scope project, demands seat-by-seat oversight, treats the work as a deliverable instead of an outcome.
- **Too cheap for the problem** — the budget can't fund a real solution. Doing it anyway means you do a half-job and own the result.

**Bad clients eat the time, credibility, and energy you need for good ones.** Declining is a long-term move. The good client you didn't have capacity for is the cost of the bad client you kept.

## Decline well

When you decline, three rules:

1. **Name what's not working** — briefly, factually, without making them the villain. "I don't think I'm the right fit for this because [specific reason — fit, capacity, expertise mismatch]."
2. **Refer if you can** — "You might try [person / firm] who specializes in [thing]." If you don't have a referral, say so honestly.
3. **No bridge-burning, no over-explaining.** One paragraph. They don't need your full reasoning; they need a clean answer they can act on. Long explanations read as guilty hedging.

## Anti-patterns

- **Defensive answers** — every question becomes a justification. Vendor mode.
- **Endless agreement** — yes to every ask, no push-back, no point of view. Yes-machine mode.
- **Over-discounting** — the race-to-the-bottom that turns the engagement into a loss before it starts.
- **Apologizing for your price** — pre-discounts it. State it flat.
- **Taking a bad-fit engagement anyway** — "I need the money this month" is real but expensive. The next month you'd have spent finding a good fit becomes the next year you spend recovering from a bad one.
- **Declining badly** — long explanations, blaming the client, drifting communications. A clean no is a gift.

Pair with `discover` (peer-mode starts in the first conversation), `propose` (peer-mode shows up in how you present options), `price` (peer-mode is the spine to hold the number), and `engagement-shape` (peer-mode picks the shape that fits, not the shape they asked for).
