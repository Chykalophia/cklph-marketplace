# scope — define done by the outcome

Scope is what the client agrees the work is. If you define it by hours or deliverables, you've sold a commodity. Define it by the **business result** and you're selling judgment.

## The format

For every engagement, surface five things — in writing, the client signs off:

- **Outcome** — the measurable business result. "Ops team makes weekly decisions from a single dashboard," not "3 dashboards." Phrase it so the client could explain it to their board in one sentence.
- **Inclusions** — what's in. Specific enough that both sides know.
- **Exclusions** — what's *not* in. Explicit. "Not included — data migration from legacy CRM, training beyond admin handoff, ongoing support after sign-off." If you don't write exclusions, you've written open scope, and scope creep is now guaranteed.
- **Success criteria** — how both sides know you hit the outcome. Often a small number of observable signals: "Weekly ops standup uses the dashboard for at least 4 consecutive weeks without manual spreadsheet."
- **Acceptance signal** — what the client does to say we're done. A meeting, a sign-off, a final review. Without this, engagements drift indefinitely.

## Hours are your problem, not theirs

How long it takes you, how many people, what stack — those are *your* operating concerns. The client doesn't buy hours; they buy the result. If you find yourself writing "approximately 40 hours over 6 weeks" in client-facing language, you've leaked your internal accounting into their decision frame. Keep it internal.

## When scope is fuzzy, name the assumption

Don't paper over uncertainty. Write it as a stated assumption the client confirms or rejects:

> "Assumption — your current CRM data is exportable as CSV without IT involvement. If this is wrong, that pulls in a small migration phase before we can start."

That sentence either gets nodded through (and you're protected) or surfaces a problem now (when it's cheap) instead of mid-engagement (when it's expensive).

## Mid-engagement scope changes

"Can you also do X?" is a feature of every good engagement, not a problem. Handle it the same way: name the new outcome, name what it adds (inclusions + exclusions), name what it changes about the original scope or timeline, and price it as its own decision. Don't absorb it silently — that's how you train clients to expect it for free.

## Anti-patterns

- **Deliverable-as-outcome** — "3 dashboards, a runbook, and 2 training sessions" tells the client nothing about whether their business problem got solved.
- **Time-as-outcome** — "40 hours of consulting" sells hours; you wanted to sell results.
- **No exclusions** — open scope. Every "wait, I assumed that was included" is a hit to margin and trust.
- **Silent scope absorption** — saying yes to every "while you're at it" without surfacing the cost. Looks generous; trains the relationship to undervalue you.

Pair with `discover` (precedes — you can't scope what you haven't diagnosed), `propose` (turns scope into options), `price` (turns outcome into a number), and `position` (when scope conversations start feeling adversarial).
