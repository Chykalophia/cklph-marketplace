# propose — options, never an estimate

A single number is a commodity quote. The client compares it to other numbers and you've already lost the conversation. **Always offer 2–3 tiered options.** Three makes you think harder; one makes the client argue, three makes them choose.

## Why options

- **Anchors comparison.** Three options compared to each other beats one option compared to "no thanks."
- **Demonstrates judgment.** Picking which three options to offer *is* the consulting work — it shows you understood the discovery.
- **Gives the client agency.** They pick what kind of result they want, not whether to buy at all.

## Tier the *outcome*, not the size

This is the move most people get wrong. Bad tiers are S/M/L of the same deliverable — "20 hours / 40 hours / 60 hours of the same thing." That's still selling hours; the client just picks the cheapest.

Good tiers are **distinct outcomes** — each one a real choice about how much of the problem to solve, how durably, and with what level of commitment. Examples:

- **Tier 1** — diagnose and document; client implements. (Lower price, lower lift, no execution risk on us.)
- **Tier 2** — diagnose, document, and implement the core. (Mid price, shared execution.)
- **Tier 3** — full ownership end-to-end including measurement and handoff. (Higher price, we carry the outcome.)

Each tier should feel like a different *answer* to the client's question, not a different *size* of the same answer.

## Structure

1. **Outcome restated** at the top — one paragraph, in their language. Shows you listened in discovery.
2. **Options grouped by value tier** — for each: what they get, what's excluded, what success looks like, what it costs. Use the `scope` format inside each tier.
3. **One concrete next step** — "If Tier 2 is the right shape, the next move is a kickoff call by Friday; here's a calendar link." Not "let me know what you think." Proposals that end in vague invitations sit forever.

## Cap at three. Maybe two.

More than three options is decision paralysis. The client can't compare four things at once; they delay, then go cold. Two clean tiers often beats three muddled ones. If you can't articulate a real third tier, don't invent one.

## Anti-patterns

- **Single estimate** — turns you into a vendor in a comparison shop.
- **More than 4 options** — paralysis; they delay; deal goes cold.
- **Same-thing-different-size** — you're still selling hours; you've just dressed them up.
- **No next step** — proposals without a concrete next move just sit.
- **Hiding the price** — "let's discuss on a call" reads as evasive. Put real numbers next to each tier.
- **Apologizing for the high tier** — naming it "premium" or "if budget allows" pre-discounts it. Present it flat.

Pair with `discover` (the source material for which options to offer), `scope` (format inside each tier), `price` (how to set the numbers), `position` (peer-not-vendor framing throughout), and `engagement-shape` (so each tier reflects the right shape — project / retainer / advisor / fractional).
